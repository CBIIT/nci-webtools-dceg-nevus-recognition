Moles, Dysplastic Nevi & Melanoma

Dysplastic nevi (DN) are atypical moles. DN are important risk markers for melanoma, and precursor lesions for some melanomas.

This web-based educational tool includes photographs of 30 different DN. Epidemiologists, clinicians and staff at the National Cancer Institute developed the tool to help patients and other individuals recognize DN and melanomas that started in DN. The photographs were taken of participants in the NCI Clinical, Laboratory, and Epidemiologic Characterization of Individuals and Families at High Risk of Melanoma.

Nearly all 30 of the DN and melanomas were photographed over the course of many years. The pictures and accompanying clinical descriptions show how pigmented lesions evolve in this population of U.S. melanoma-prone families. The types of moles shown include normal, common acquired moles, dysplastic nevi (DN), and some melanomas that arose in DN.

About the Tool

The Moles, Dysplastic Nevi & Melanoma Tool presents photographs in three main groups of pigmented lesions: common moles; dysplastic nevi (DN); and melanomas that arose from DN.

The dysplastic nevi section is subdivided into: stable and fading DN, and DN evolving toward melanoma.

The case series show changes in individual pigmented lesions over a number of years and across the spectrum of changes typically seen in U.S. melanoma-prone families. As a teaching aid, each case series includes a description of the “ABCDE” features for each type of pigmented lesion (moles, DN, and melanomas). Although the “ABCDE” rules were made for identifying early melanoma, they can also be used to describe DN.

The tool also contains additional information and links to other resources about dysplastic nevi and the prevention and early detection of melanoma.

What is the "ABCDE" rule?

The "ABCDE" rule describes the features of early melanoma. These features are:

    • Asymmetry - The shape of one half does not match the other half.
    • Border that is irregular - The edges are often ragged, notched, or blurred in outline. The pigment may spread into the surrounding skin.
    • Color that is uneven - Shades of black, brown, and tan may be present. Areas of white, gray, red, pink, or blue may also be seen.
    • Diameter - There is a change in size, usually an increase. Melanomas can be tiny, but most are larger than 6 millimeters wide (about 1/4 inch wide).
    • Evolving - The mole has changed over the past few weeks or months.

Disclaimer

  • The photographs of the moles and melanomas used in this tool were taken over more than a 35-year period from participants enrolled in the NCI Familial Melanoma Study.
  • The pictures show examples of the variability in pigmented lesions in U.S. melanoma-prone families.
  • Because most of the study participants are Caucasians, the nevi and melanomas shown are not representative of nevi and melanomas in individuals with darker skin.
  • Melanomas and lesions suspicious for melanoma vary widely in appearance; thus, the pictures should not be used to diagnose melanoma.
  • NCI does not provide specific medical advice to users of its website.
  • NCI urges users to consult with a qualified health care provider for diagnosis and for answers to their personal medical questions.

About the photos

  • The photographs will have variations in color due to the differences in photography equipment, lighting, and skin color of the individual (e.g. sunburned or suntanned).
  • Photographs are standardized to ease viewing.
  • Rulers in each photograph address the variations in relative size of the moles and melanomas. The unit of measurement for these rulers are millimeters.
